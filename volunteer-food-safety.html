Temperature Control & the Danger Zone

Understanding the "danger zone" and proper temperature control is crucial for preventing bacterial growth in food. Time and temperature abuse is the leading cause of foodborne illness.

üö® The Danger Zone: 4¬∞C - 60¬∞C (40¬∞F - 140¬∞F)

Bacteria multiply rapidly between these temperatures. Food should not remain in the danger zone for more than 2 hours (or 1 hour if temperature is above 32¬∞C/90¬∞F).

  • Refrigerate perishable foods immediately
  • Keep refrigerated foods at 4¬∞C or below
  • Use ice packs during transport
  • Monitor refrigerator temperatures regularly

  • Keep hot foods steaming hot
  • Use insulated containers for transport

Safe Food Handling Practices

Proper handling techniques prevent cross-contamination and maintain food safety from collection through distribution.

üßº Personal Hygiene

Hand Washing

  • Wash hands for 20 seconds with soap
  • Wash before handling food and after any contamination
  • Use single-use paper towels for drying
  • Wash hands after using restroom, sneezing, or touching face

Health Requirements

  • Report any illness symptoms immediately
  • Stay home if experiencing vomiting or diarrhea
  • Cover cuts and wounds with waterproof bandages
  • Wear clean clothing and closed-toe shoes

üîÑ Preventing Cross-Contamination

Separate Raw & Ready-to-Eat

  • Use separate cutting boards for raw meat and produce
  • Store raw meat below ready-to-eat foods
  • Use separate containers for different food types
  • Wash equipment between different food types

Sanitize Surfaces

  • Clean and sanitize all food contact surfaces
  • Use food-safe sanitizers (100ppm chlorine or equivalent)
  • Allow sanitizers to air dry
  • Sanitize transport vehicles and containers

Inspecting Donated Food

Learning to properly inspect donated food ensures only safe, high-quality items are distributed to recipients.

1

Visual Inspection

Check for obvious signs of spoilage or contamination

  • Look for mold, discoloration, or unusual odors
  • Check packaging for damage or swelling
  • Examine food for sliminess or unusual texture
  • Verify packaging is intact and properly sealed
2

Temperature Check

Ensure food has been maintained at safe temperatures

  • Check temperature of perishable items immediately
  • Verify cold chain has been maintained
  • Document temperatures on collection
  • Reject items that have been in danger zone too long
3

Date & Label Check

Verify expiration dates and proper labeling

  • Check "use by" and "best by" dates
  • Verify proper allergen labeling
  • Ensure clear ingredient lists
  • Document any date extensions for appropriate items

‚úÖ Accept vs ‚ùå Reject Guidelines

Generally Safe to Accept:

  • Fresh produce (even if slightly imperfect)
  • Sealed packaged goods within date
  • Bakery items from same day
  • Canned goods (check for dents/bulging)
  • Dairy products within safe temperature range

Always Reject:

  • Food from unauthorized sources
  • Items that have been in danger zone >2 hours
  • Food with signs of mold or spoilage
  • Damaged cans or swollen packaging
  • Items without proper labeling

Activity: Food Safety Scenario Practice

Practice applying food safety principles to real-world scenarios you might encounter as a volunteer.

How to Complete This Activity:

  1. Read each scenario carefully
  2. Identify potential food safety risks
  3. Determine the appropriate action to take
  4. Explain your reasoning for each decision
  5. Consider how you would prevent similar situations

Scenario 1: Restaurant Pickup

You arrive at a restaurant to pick up prepared meals. The containers are warm but not hot, and the restaurant manager says they've been holding for 3 hours. The internal temperature reads 45¬∞C.

Scenario 2: Temperature Emergency

During transport, your vehicle breaks down in hot weather. You have perishable food that has been in the danger zone for 5 hours. The ice packs have melted.

Scenario 3: Suspicious Donation

A donor offers you homemade food items without proper labeling. They seem hesitant to provide ingredient information and the food looks like it may have been stored improperly.
